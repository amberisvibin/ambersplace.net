the folks who made Glaze, which lets you protect your art from being reproduced by e.g. Stable Diffusion and other text-to-image programs, have made a new tool called Nightshade. Nightshade works similarly to Glaze - by making adjustments to images that are imperceptible to users but which modify an "ai art" program's ability to ingest them - but instead of simply preventing reproduction, Nightshade actually "poisons" training data, creating bad associations between words that make the tool less useful. The stated goal is to disincentivize mass image scraping, and to encourage the companies that make these tools to actually get permission for the images they train their models on.

Lexie_Plays_Fast@Lexie-Plays-Fast10/24/2023, 7:37 PM

It also cannot possibly work.

Fundamentally.

I've seen some of what the Glaze & Nightshade does, and what it does is, in simple terms, adding visual noise to the image that's generally imperceptible to the human eye.

The obvious problem with this approach is that you cannot add anything to a picture that a) is not very obvious to the human eye and b) is not trivially removable by, say, resampling the image as a q=0.9 JPEG or whatever.

And sure, if every single artist uses Glaze & Nightshade or whatever, then every model sampling images will have to do that extra resampling step, which… victory?

Also, having seen some of the stuff Glaze adds, it's actually not imperceptible at all. It's obvious visual noise. In other words, what Glaze does is degrade the image for the people the art is ostensibly for without really impacting the people you want to "not see" it.

Lexie_Plays_Fast@Lexie-Plays-Fast10/24/2023, 8:21 PM

It's not so much a matter of what we know how to do, there is a hard limit to how much info you can hide inside an image without either distorting the image or increasing the file size by a suspicious amount.

And like I said, Glaze definitely creates visual noise! Granted, the examples I've seen are CGI renders, not photos, but even so, the effect is apparent.

The basic functionality of Nightshade seems to essentially do what you're describing, e.g. by hiding, by steganography, an image of a dog inside of an image of a cat, in a way that the specific input filters used by whatever model you're trying to poison will see the dog, while humans looking at the image will see a cat.

A lot of classifier models are susceptible to attacks like these, where as little as a single (extremely carefully chosen) pixel set to a specific (extremely carefully chosen) colour will profoundly confuse the classifier and give entirely wrong results. Still, it's important to understand that these are not strictly speaking weaknesses of the software used, they are due to specific shapes in the data in the model, and the result of lots of artists using Glaze & Nightshade on their art will not, unfortunately, be tech bros giving up, but rather effort being put into defeating whatever the software does.

Cynically, one could even posit that Glaze & Nightshade as a project might exist to probe the limits of what kind of quality degradation people will accept in the pursuit of "defeating AI" or whatever, but that borders on the conspiratorial.
